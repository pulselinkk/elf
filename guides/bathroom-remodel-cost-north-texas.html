A bathroom is a small room with expensive failure points.

Tile size, waterproofing system, shower glass, vanity size, and plumbing moves are the cost drivers. Hidden damage can change the scope after demo.

A bathroom remodel can be a simple cosmetic upgrade or a moisture-control project hiding behind tile. In North Texas, slab movement, hard water, poor builder ventilation, and failed shower pans are common reasons a bathroom costs more than the first glance suggests.

The goal is not just a nicer bathroom. It is a room that drains correctly, dries correctly, resists mold, and still looks clean after years of daily use.

The ranges below separate vanity refreshes, tub-to-shower conversions, tile replacements, and full primary bath rebuilds so you can decide how deep the scope should go.

Field note: Best budget move: fix water management before buying premium fixtures. A beautiful shower over weak waterproofing is the most expensive kind of remodel to redo.

What moves the price.

Bathroom estimates move when the unseen parts change.

Waterproofing

The shower system is the project.

The pan, wall membrane, curb, niche, and valve penetrations all need a complete water strategy.

Never judge by tile alone
Tile labor

Large tile is not always cheaper.

Large-format tile needs flatter walls, better leveling, careful cuts, and more precise layout around niches and drains.

Prep determines finish quality
Plumbing

Moving drains is expensive on slabs.

Many DFW bathrooms are on concrete slabs. Relocating a drain can involve concrete cutting and patching.

Layout changes add risk
Ventilation

Moisture control saves the remodel.

A quiet, properly ducted fan matters in North Texas humidity. Paint and grout last longer when the room dries quickly.

Small cost, big protection
Glass

Shower glass is a real line item.

Door height, hardware finish, panel size, and custom measurements can move the price by thousands.

$1.2K-$4.5K common range
Damage

Old leaks change the plan.

Soft subfloor, loose tile, swollen trim, and stained drywall should be priced as repair work, not covered up.

Inspect during demo

North Texas bathroom notes.

Local conditions, buyer expectations, climate, and typical construction methods change how a project should be planned in North Texas.

Local planning notes
  • Hard water around Frisco, Plano, and McKinney makes glass and dark grout harder to maintain unless products are chosen carefully.
  • Older Denton homes can have framing and subfloor surprises around tubs, especially where old caulk failed.
  • Large primary baths in newer suburbs often need better lighting more than more tile. Dim builder lighting makes new finishes underperform.
  • If a bathroom shares a wall with another wet room, inspect both sides before closing walls.
Before you approve a quote

Ask whether the proposal includes protection, material handling, disposal, prep, finish details, and cleanup. The missing line items are usually where budgets get messy.

Good rule

If two quotes are far apart, compare scope line by line before assuming one contractor is simply cheaper.

How the work should unfold.

A clean sequence protects the home, reduces rework, and keeps decisions from arriving too late.

Moisture and layout review

Check visible water damage, grout failure, ventilation, plumbing access, and desired shower or vanity layout.

Selections and waterproofing plan

Choose tile, grout, fixtures, glass direction, niche location, fan, and membrane approach.

Demo and hidden repair

Remove finishes, inspect framing and subfloor, correct plumbing, and prepare flat waterproof surfaces.

Tile, trim, glass, and finish

Install tile, grout, fixtures, vanity, mirrors, lighting, paint, accessories, and final sealant.

Bathroom planning checklist.

Use this before requesting a quote or buying materials. It keeps the conversation concrete.

Checklist
  • Photograph every leak stain before demo.
  • Choose grout color with hard water maintenance in mind.
  • Decide niche size before tile layout starts.
  • Confirm whether the fan vents outdoors, not into the attic.
  • Price shower glass early because it affects the final budget.
  • Keep a contingency for hidden framing, subfloor, or plumbing damage.
Do not skip
  • Do not install tile over a questionable shower pan.
  • Do not skip waterproofing behind niches or benches.
  • Do not assume a tub-to-shower conversion is simple on a slab.
  • Do not choose glossy floor tile without checking slip resistance.
Estimate tip

Ask for the finish standard.

Every quote should define what counts as complete: cleanup, touch-ups, paint blending, material disposal, and what happens if hidden damage appears.

Frequently asked questions.

Short answers to the questions homeowners ask before they commit to the scope.

What is a realistic bathroom remodel budget in North Texas?

Small powder rooms can start around $2,500. Full hall baths commonly land between $7,000 and $22,000. Primary bath remodels often run $18,000 to $48,000 or more.

Is a tub-to-shower conversion worth it?

It can be, especially in a primary bath. For resale, many homes should keep at least one tub somewhere in the house if families are the likely buyer.

What is the biggest hidden cost?

Water damage. Failed caulk, loose tile, old shower pans, and soft subfloor can add repair work before finishes can be installed.

Can ELF help with only tile or only the vanity?

Yes. The scope can be a full remodel or a specific piece like shower tile, vanity installation, trim, paint, or fixture updates.
