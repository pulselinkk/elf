Texas Problem Guide - Foundation movement - DFW

Foundation movement and drywall cracks in Texas need calm diagnosis first. Repair what moved.

Drywall cracks are one of the most common homeowner worries in North Texas. Some are ordinary seasonal movement from clay soil. Some deserve a foundation or structural evaluation before anyone patches the wall. This guide explains how to read the crack pattern, choose the right repair method, and avoid hiding a movement problem under fresh texture.

Start with the real cause.

Texas slabs move because the soil below them moves. Dry seasons shrink the clay; wet seasons swell it. Drywall is rigid, so stress shows up around door corners, window corners, ceiling lines, and tape seams. The question is not whether a crack exists. The question is whether it is stable enough to repair cosmetically.

ELF separates drywall repair from foundation diagnosis. We can repair cracks, texture, and paint, but cracks with major displacement, rapid growth, exterior brick patterns, or doors that suddenly stop working may need an engineer before finish work. Cosmetic repair should not hide active structural concerns.

Field note: The best drywall crack repair is wider than the crack itself. Narrow smears fail because they do not bridge movement, feather texture, or blend paint across visible light.

Choose the right level of repair.

Most home problems have three possible paths: maintain, repair, or rebuild. Picking the wrong tier is how small projects get expensive.

Stable hairline cracks

Cosmetic patch

V-cut, tape if needed, setting compound, feather, texture match, prime, and paint.

Crack came back

Recurring crack repair

Use stronger tape, wider float, better primer, and check if movement is still active.

Structural warning signs

Evaluate first

Pause cosmetic work and bring in qualified foundation or engineering advice before repair.

When to slow down

If the problem involves active water, suspected hazardous material, structural movement, electrical risk, or a rule-controlled exterior change, pause the cosmetic work until the cause and approval path are clear.

How to handle it cleanly.

This is the field sequence that keeps a home repair from becoming a repeat repair.

Photograph and measure

Record crack width, location, and whether doors or windows are affected.

Screen for warning signs

Check offset, rapid growth, exterior cracks, floor slope, and sticking doors.

Repair the finish

Use proper tape, compound, feathering, texture match, primer, and paint strategy.

Monitor after seasons

Watch the repair through dry and wet cycles before assuming movement is solved.

Homeowner checklist.

Use this before requesting an estimate. Better photos and better context make the first quote conversation much more useful.

Before you book
  • Measure cracks with a ruler before repair.
  • Do not patch over a crack that is actively widening.
  • Check exterior brick near matching interior cracks.
  • Expect texture and paint blending to take more area than the crack itself.
  • Improve drainage before blaming every crack on drywall workmanship.
Estimate tip

Send context, not just close-ups.

Include one wide room or exterior photo, one close-up, measurements if possible, the city, when it started, and whether it changes with weather or use.

Decision rule

Do not pay twice for the same symptom.

If the first repair failed quickly, the second plan should explain why it failed and what will be different this time.

Frequently asked questions.

Short answers for homeowners deciding whether to clean, repair, rebuild, or call a specialist first.

Are drywall cracks in Texas always foundation problems?

No. Many are cosmetic or seasonal. The pattern, width, growth rate, offset, and related symptoms decide how seriously to treat them.

Can a drywall crack be repaired permanently?

Stable cracks can often be repaired for long-lasting results. Active movement can reopen any cosmetic repair.

When should I call a foundation engineer?

Call for wide cracks, offset cracks, rapid change, exterior stair-step cracks, sloping floors, or doors and windows that suddenly stop operating.

Why did my last drywall patch fail?

It may have been patched too narrowly, painted without proper primer, repaired with the wrong compound, or applied before movement stabilized.
