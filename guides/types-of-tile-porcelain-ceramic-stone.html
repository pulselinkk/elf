Start with the real decision.

Porcelain, ceramic, stone, mosaic, and large-format tile each have different installation needs, maintenance demands, and best-use rooms.

Tile should be chosen for room use, substrate, water exposure, slip resistance, cleaning, and the amount of visible pattern. A beautiful tile can become a problem when used in the wrong place.

In North Texas, hard water, slab movement, and shower waterproofing make tile planning more technical than a showroom display suggests.

Field note: A finish should pass three tests: close-up detail, room-wide balance, and how it looks in ordinary daylight.

Read the tile spec before the showroom sample wins.

Tile material choice should account for absorption, edge type, slip feel, size tolerance, cutting difficulty, and the surface beneath it.

Porcelain is usually denser and better for wet or high-traffic rooms, but that does not mean every porcelain tile is easy to install. Large-format porcelain can demand flatter walls, stronger setting materials, leveling clips, better saw work, and a layout plan that avoids skinny cuts at doors or tubs.

Ceramic is often excellent for backsplashes and wall tile because it cuts cleaner and can cost less. Natural stone brings variation and depth, but it also brings sealing, etching, staining, and cleaning limitations that should be accepted before the first box is opened.

Floor rating

Ask where it can live.

Wall tile on a floor can fail fast. Check whether the product is intended for floors, showers, exterior exposure, or only dry vertical surfaces.

Flatness

Big tile magnifies small waves.

Large pieces do not bend to a wavy slab or bowed wall. Prep time, patching, and layout control matter more as tile size increases.

Edges

Rectified changes the joint.

Straighter tile edges can support tighter joints, but they also make lippage and substrate problems easier to see. Handmade edges need a different expectation.

Wet safety

Shower floors need grip and slope.

Small mosaics often follow shower pan slope better than large tile. Smooth polished tile can look premium and still be the wrong underfoot choice.

Frequently asked questions.

Short answers for deciding whether this is DIY, maintenance, repair, or a professional project.

Is porcelain better than ceramic?

Porcelain is denser and often more durable, but ceramic can be excellent for walls and backsplashes.

Does natural stone need sealing?

Usually yes, and it needs different cleaning habits than porcelain.

Is large-format tile harder to install?

Yes, because substrate flatness, lippage control, and layout are more demanding.

What tile is best for shower floors?

Small-format mosaic with appropriate slip resistance is commonly used because it follows slope better.
