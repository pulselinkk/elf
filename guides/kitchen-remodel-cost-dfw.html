Most kitchen budgets are decided before demolition.

These ranges include common labor and ordinary materials for DFW homes. Premium appliance packages, designer slabs, engineering, and city permit fees can raise the final number.

In DFW, kitchen remodel pricing is not one number. A well-planned cosmetic refresh can make a 2005 builder kitchen look current for under $12,000, while a wall-removal remodel with new cabinetry and appliance upgrades can climb past $90,000 fast.

The biggest mistake homeowners make is pricing a kitchen by square footage alone. The real drivers are cabinet strategy, countertop material, whether the layout changes, and whether electrical, gas, or plumbing must move.

Use the ranges below as a planning tool, then narrow them with a walkthrough. ELF quotes flat scopes, so the final proposal is based on the actual kitchen rather than a vague hourly promise.

Field note: Best budget move: keep the layout if it works. Reusing the plumbing wall, preserving the cabinet footprint, and upgrading visible finishes usually beats a larger rebuild when resale value is the main goal.

What moves the price.

The same kitchen footprint can swing tens of thousands of dollars based on these choices.

Cabinets

Cabinet strategy is the largest lever.

Painting existing boxes saves money when the layout works. Replacing cabinets makes sense when boxes are damaged, door style is dated, or storage is inefficient.

35-45% of many full remodel budgets
Counters

Stone choice changes both cost and risk.

Quartz is predictable and durable. Quartzite and marble need more careful fabrication, more slab planning, and more conversation about staining and maintenance.

$55-$150+ per sqft installed
Structure

Moving walls changes the project category.

A beam, engineering, inspections, floor repair, drywall, and texture matching turn a finish job into a construction project.

Adds weeks, not just dollars
Electrical

Older kitchens often need code cleanup.

GFCI spacing, dedicated appliance circuits, old junction boxes, and under-cabinet lighting are common DFW scope additions.

Plan before cabinets are ordered
Sequencing

Lead times matter more than labor speed.

Cabinets, slabs, and specialty fixtures should be ordered before demo when possible. The best schedule is boring and deliberate.

Cabinets often lead by 4-8 weeks
Finish level

Small details create the premium feel.

Panel-ready ends, soft-close hardware, aligned reveals, caulked trim, and tile layout planning are where the kitchen starts to look custom.

High impact, easy to miss

DFW kitchen notes.

Local conditions, buyer expectations, climate, and typical construction methods change how a project should be planned in North Texas.

Local planning notes
  • Frisco and Plano homes often have open first floors where wall work may involve larger spans and careful dust control.
  • Denton and Lewisville kitchens commonly benefit from cabinet painting because older boxes are often solid wood and worth saving.
  • Homes built in the early 2000s often need lighting updates more than extra cabinetry. Poor light makes a fresh kitchen still feel old.
  • If resale is within two years, avoid over-personalized stone, extreme cabinet colors, and luxury appliance packages that outpace the neighborhood.
Before you approve a quote

Ask whether the proposal includes protection, material handling, disposal, prep, finish details, and cleanup. The missing line items are usually where budgets get messy.

Good rule

If two quotes are far apart, compare scope line by line before assuming one contractor is simply cheaper.

How the work should unfold.

A clean sequence protects the home, reduces rework, and keeps decisions from arriving too late.

Walkthrough and scope plan

Measure cabinets, check utility locations, photograph existing conditions, and identify what must stay.

Selections and pricing

Lock cabinet approach, countertop material, backsplash, fixtures, lighting, and appliance sizes before the proposal is finalized.

Ordering and prep

Order long-lead items, protect floors, schedule trades, and confirm permits if electrical, plumbing, or structure changes.

Build and finish

Complete demo, rough work, cabinets, counters, tile, paint, hardware, punch list, and final cleanup in the right order.

Kitchen planning checklist.

Use this before requesting a quote or buying materials. It keeps the conversation concrete.

Checklist
  • Photograph every cabinet wall before emptying the kitchen.
  • Decide whether the current layout actually fails or only looks dated.
  • Choose appliance sizes before cabinet orders are placed.
  • Budget a temporary cooking setup if the kitchen will be down for more than one week.
  • Confirm who handles permits, inspections, and utility shutoffs.
  • Keep at least 10 percent contingency for hidden electrical, water, or floor issues.
Do not skip
  • Do not order counters before cabinet layout is final.
  • Do not remove a wall without confirming whether it is load bearing.
  • Do not pick tile only from a close-up sample. View it across the full wall size.
  • Do not chase luxury appliances if the cabinet and lighting plan is still weak.
Estimate tip

Ask for the finish standard.

Every quote should define what counts as complete: cleanup, touch-ups, paint blending, material disposal, and what happens if hidden damage appears.

Frequently asked questions.

Short answers to the questions homeowners ask before they commit to the scope.

What is the realistic minimum for a kitchen remodel in DFW?

For a visible kitchen refresh, plan on $4,500 to $12,000. That usually means cabinet paint or repairs, hardware, backsplash, lighting touch-ups, and small fixture swaps. A true rebuild starts much higher.

Can I keep my cabinets and still make the kitchen look new?

Yes, if the boxes are solid, the layout works, and the door style is not fighting the design. Painting, hardware, counters, and backsplash can transform a kitchen without replacing the entire cabinet system.

When does a kitchen need permits?

Cosmetic work usually does not. Moving electrical, plumbing, gas, or load-bearing structure usually does. City rules vary, so the scope should be checked before demo.

What should I choose first?

Choose the cabinet plan first, then counters, backsplash, sink, faucet, lighting, and paint. Cabinets control dimensions, appliance openings, and countertop template timing.
