Texas Problem Guide - Exterior approvals - DFW

HOA-approved improvements in DFW start with documentation, not drama. Plan before you paint.

Many North DFW homes sit inside HOA communities where exterior changes need approval before work starts. Paint colors, fence style, door colors, exterior lighting, visible repairs, patio changes, and even material choices can trigger review. This guide shows how to prepare a clean approval packet and avoid paying twice for work that should have been approved first.

Start with the real cause.

HOA approval is not just paperwork. It affects schedule, material ordering, color choice, and sometimes the contractor method. A homeowner may love a front door color or fence upgrade, but if the association requires a specific palette, height, stain, or board style, the project needs to respect that before work begins.

ELF helps by making the scope clear: what changes, where it is visible, what materials are used, what colors are proposed, and what finished result the HOA should expect. A clean packet reduces delays and keeps the project from turning into a repaint or rebuild.

Field note: HOA rules vary by neighborhood. Treat this guide as planning advice, then check your own covenants, architectural guidelines, and approval process before work begins.

Choose the right level of repair.

Most home problems have three possible paths: maintain, repair, or rebuild. Picking the wrong tier is how small projects get expensive.

Like-for-like

Simple repair

Replacing damaged trim, fence boards, or paint with matching material may be fastest, but still verify rules.

Color or style change

Visible refresh

Exterior paint, door color, shutters, fence stain, and visible fixtures need a clear submittal.

Structure or layout

Larger improvement

Pergolas, patio covers, screening, and additions usually require drawings, dimensions, and longer review.

North Texas notes.

DFW homes have their own repair patterns because of clay soil, heat, storms, hard water, fast construction, and HOA communities.

Local planning notes
  • Frisco, Allen, Plano, McKinney, and Southlake neighborhoods often have detailed exterior guidelines.
  • Newer master-planned communities may be stricter about fences, colors, and visible additions.
  • After storms, emergency stabilization may happen quickly, but permanent replacements may still need standard approval.
When to slow down

If the problem involves active water, suspected hazardous material, structural movement, electrical risk, or a rule-controlled exterior change, pause the cosmetic work until the cause and approval path are clear.

How to handle it cleanly.

This is the field sequence that keeps a home repair from becoming a repeat repair.

Read the rules

Find the architectural guidelines and identify what needs approval.

Build the packet

Collect photos, color names, material specs, drawings, dimensions, and contractor scope.

Wait before ordering

Do not buy custom materials or schedule finish work until approval is clear.

Document completion

Keep approval, receipts, photos, and final finish notes for future resale or questions.

Homeowner checklist.

Use this before requesting an estimate. Better photos and better context make the first quote conversation much more useful.

Before you book
  • Take wide photos of the front, side, and affected area.
  • Use exact paint brand, color name, number, and sheen in the request.
  • Include fence height, stain, picket style, and gate details.
  • Check whether the city also requires a permit for the scope.
  • Keep written approval before work begins.
Estimate tip

Send context, not just close-ups.

Include one wide room or exterior photo, one close-up, measurements if possible, the city, when it started, and whether it changes with weather or use.

Decision rule

Do not pay twice for the same symptom.

If the first repair failed quickly, the second plan should explain why it failed and what will be different this time.

Frequently asked questions.

Short answers for homeowners deciding whether to clean, repair, rebuild, or call a specialist first.

Do all HOA repairs need approval?

No, but visible exterior changes often do. Like-for-like repairs may be simpler, yet every association is different.

Can a contractor submit to the HOA for me?

Sometimes, but the homeowner is usually responsible for approval. A contractor can provide scope, photos, material notes, and drawings to support the request.

What happens if I paint before approval?

You may be required to repaint or modify the work. That is why approval should happen before materials and labor are committed.

Are city permits and HOA approval the same thing?

No. A city permit and HOA approval are separate. Some projects may need both, one, or neither depending on scope and location.
