This guide is about sanding risk and irreversible mistakes.

DIY sanding is a different decision from routine maintenance because one uneven pass can remove too much wood or leave permanent chatter marks.

A floor-sanding project asks whether the homeowner can control a drum sander, edger, grit sequence, dust containment, stain testing, finish compatibility, and transitions at walls and closets. The wrong machine angle can create waves that are visible forever.

The truth is not that DIY is impossible. It is that sanding belongs to homeowners who can practice, accept risk, and understand how thin the wear layer may already be. Engineered floors, pet stains, deep water marks, and unknown previous refinishes make the decision much sharper.

Solid hardwood has more forgiveness than many engineered floors, but every previous sanding reduces the margin.

Frequently asked questions.

Short answers for deciding whether this is DIY, maintenance, repair, or a professional project.

Can I sand hardwood floors myself?

Technically yes, but the risk is high, especially in visible rooms or unknown flooring.

Can engineered hardwood be sanded?

Sometimes, depending on wear layer thickness. Some cannot be sanded safely.

Will sanding remove pet stains?

Not always. Deep stains may remain or require board replacement.

Is screen and recoat different from sanding?

Yes. It refreshes the finish without removing as much wood, when the floor condition allows.
