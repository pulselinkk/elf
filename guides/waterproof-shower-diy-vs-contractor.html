This guide is built for North Texas homeowners who want a practical answer before buying materials, booking work, or starting a weekend project. The best path depends on condition, risk, finish expectations, and how expensive the mistake would be if the first plan fails.

Use the sections below as a decision tool. If the page points to a professional repair, that usually means the hidden layer, water path, safety risk, or finish standard matters more than the visible surface.

North Texas notes.

DFW homes have specific repair and design patterns because of heat, clay soil, storms, hard water, humidity, slabs, and resale expectations.

Local planning notes
  • North Texas slab and second-floor showers both suffer when water escapes the wet area.
  • Hard water and humidity make maintenance important after install.
  • Primary showers in Frisco and Southlake often justify professional waterproofing.
Slow down when needed

Active water, structural symptoms, unsafe access, electrical risk, or hidden waterproofing should be solved before cosmetic finish work.

Homeowner checklist.

Use this before starting the project or asking for an estimate.

Checklist
  • Do not rely on grout as waterproofing.
  • Use compatible drain and membrane products.
  • Slope horizontal surfaces to drain.
  • Waterproof niches and curbs carefully.
  • Stop if instructions are unclear.
Estimate tip

Send context, not only close-ups.

Include one wide photo, one close-up, rough measurements, the city, when the issue started, and what has already been tried.

Decision rule

Protect the expensive layer.

When water, substrate, structure, or finish quality is involved, solve that layer before spending on visible finishes.

Frequently asked questions.

Short answers for deciding whether this is DIY, maintenance, repair, or a professional project.

Can I waterproof a shower myself?

Possible, but high-risk. Follow one complete manufacturer system exactly.

Is grout waterproof?

No. Grout is a finish joint, not the waterproofing layer.

What is the most common shower failure?

Corners, curbs, niches, pans, and penetrations are common failure points.

Is a contractor worth it for showers?

Often yes because failures are expensive and hidden.
