Cabinet paint is a finish solution, not a layout solution.

Cabinet costs depend on linear footage, door count, finish method, box condition, hardware, trim, and whether countertops must be removed.

Cabinet painting can transform a kitchen for a fraction of replacement cost, but it does not fix a bad layout, weak boxes, poor storage, swollen panels, or door styles that look dated even after paint.

Replacement costs more because it changes the entire cabinet system: boxes, doors, fillers, panels, trim, hardware, installation, and often counters or backsplash.

Use this guide to decide whether to paint, replace, reface, or combine approaches in a DFW kitchen.

Field note: Best budget move: paint when the layout works and invest in new hardware, soft-close hinges, lighting, and backsplash. Replace when the cabinet structure is the thing you dislike.

What moves the price.

The right answer is visible when you inspect the boxes, doors, and layout separately.

Boxes

Solid boxes make painting viable.

If the boxes are plywood or sturdy wood, square, and dry, painting can be a smart upgrade.

Structure first
Doors

Door style affects the result.

Flat, shaker, and simple raised-panel doors paint well. Heavy arches and dated profiles may still look dated.

Paint changes color, not shape
Layout

Bad storage is a replacement problem.

Awkward corners, dead space, poor pantry storage, and cramped appliance openings are not solved by paint.

Function matters
Finish

Prep decides durability.

Cleaning, degreasing, sanding, bonding primer, and cure time all matter more than the topcoat brand alone.

No shortcut finish
Counters

Counters connect the decision.

Replacing cabinets usually affects countertops. Painting can preserve existing counters and reduce scope.

Big budget link
ROI

Resale likes clean and current.

For pre-sale, painting often gives better return than full replacement unless the cabinets are damaged or layout is poor.

Know the goal

DFW cabinet notes.

Local conditions, buyer expectations, climate, and typical construction methods change how a project should be planned in North Texas.

Local planning notes
  • Early-2000s honey maple and dark espresso cabinets in North DFW often paint well if boxes are solid.
  • White and warm neutral cabinets are still safer resale choices than extreme colors in most Frisco, Plano, and McKinney neighborhoods.
  • Cheap cabinet paint over grease-prone kitchens fails quickly. Degreasing and bonding primer are not optional.
  • If the kitchen already needs new counters, cabinet replacement becomes more attractive because the countertop dependency is already open.
Before you approve a quote

Ask whether the proposal includes protection, material handling, disposal, prep, finish details, and cleanup. The missing line items are usually where budgets get messy.

Good rule

If two quotes are far apart, compare scope line by line before assuming one contractor is simply cheaper.

How the work should unfold.

A clean sequence protects the home, reduces rework, and keeps decisions from arriving too late.

Inspect boxes and layout

Check door count, hinges, drawer function, water damage, panel condition, and storage complaints.

Choose paint, replace, or hybrid

Compare budget, timeline, resale goal, cabinet structure, and countertop impact.

Prep or order

Painting needs cleaning and controlled finish work. Replacement needs design, measurements, and lead time.

Finish and adjust

Install hardware, adjust doors, touch up trim, and protect the finish during cure time.

Cabinet decision checklist.

Use this before requesting a quote or buying materials. It keeps the conversation concrete.

Checklist
  • Open every cabinet and inspect the box, not just the door.
  • Count doors and drawers before requesting pricing.
  • Decide whether storage is the problem or appearance is the problem.
  • Check for swelling near sink and dishwasher.
  • Choose hardware before old holes are repaired.
  • Plan cure time before heavy cabinet use.
Do not skip
  • Do not paint over grease without thorough cleaning.
  • Do not paint damaged boxes and expect replacement performance.
  • Do not replace cabinets without confirming countertop impact.
  • Do not choose a trendy color only for resale.
Estimate tip

Ask for the finish standard.

Every quote should define what counts as complete: cleanup, touch-ups, paint blending, material disposal, and what happens if hidden damage appears.

Frequently asked questions.

Short answers to the questions homeowners ask before they commit to the scope.

Is cabinet painting worth it in DFW?

Yes when the cabinet boxes are solid, the layout works, and the door style still fits the home. It is often the best pre-sale kitchen upgrade.

How long does cabinet painting last?

A properly prepped and cured finish can last years in normal residential use. Durability depends on cleaning, primer, coating, cure time, and daily wear.

When should cabinets be replaced instead?

Replace when boxes are damaged, layout is poor, storage is weak, doors are severely dated, or you are already changing countertops and kitchen layout.

Can I paint cabinets myself?

You can, but cabinet paint is less forgiving than wall paint. Degreasing, sanding, bonding primer, spray quality, and cure control determine the result.
