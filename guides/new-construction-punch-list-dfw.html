Texas Problem Guide - Punch lists - DFW

New construction in DFW still needs a serious punch list before it feels finished. Inspect before living with it.

A new home is not the same thing as a finished home. Fast-growth DFW communities often deliver beautiful houses with paint misses, door rubs, cabinet adjustments, caulk gaps, tile lippage, loose fixtures, attic access issues, drywall touch-ups, and settling cracks. This guide helps homeowners organize the punch list before the builder warranty window gets away.

Start with the real cause.

New construction punch lists are about documentation and priority. Some items should go back to the builder under warranty. Some are small homeowner upgrades. Some need monitoring through seasonal movement. The mistake is mixing everything into one vague complaint list and hoping it gets handled.

ELF helps homeowners understand what belongs on a builder list and what can be bundled later as a handyman or finish day. The goal is not to fight the builder. It is to document clearly, protect the warranty, and make the home function the way a new home should.

Field note: Take photos before furniture arrives. Empty rooms reveal drywall waves, paint flashing, scratched floors, cabinet gaps, and door swing problems that become harder to prove later.

Choose the right level of repair.

Most home problems have three possible paths: maintain, repair, or rebuild. Picking the wrong tier is how small projects get expensive.

Documented defects

Builder warranty list

Send photos, room labels, dates, and concise descriptions while warranty coverage is active.

Owner preferences

Move-in upgrade day

Shelving, hardware, fixtures, TV blocking, storage, and small changes after closing.

Settlement items

Seasonal follow-up

Monitor cracks, doors, caulk, and exterior movement after the first summer and storm season.

North Texas notes.

DFW homes have their own repair patterns because of clay soil, heat, storms, hard water, fast construction, and HOA communities.

Local planning notes
  • Frisco, Prosper, Celina, McKinney, and Little Elm growth corridors see many new homes with similar finish punch items.
  • North Texas clay movement means some settling issues appear after move-in, not during the first walkthrough.
  • Storm season can reveal drainage, fence, exterior caulk, and roof-edge issues.
When to slow down

If the problem involves active water, suspected hazardous material, structural movement, electrical risk, or a rule-controlled exterior change, pause the cosmetic work until the cause and approval path are clear.

How to handle it cleanly.

This is the field sequence that keeps a home repair from becoming a repeat repair.

Room-by-room capture

Photograph each issue with a wide room shot and a close-up.

Separate warranty from upgrades

Builder defects, owner preferences, and maintenance items should be tracked separately.

Submit clearly

Use concise descriptions, dates, and locations so the builder can route repairs.

Bundle post-closing fixes

After builder items are resolved, group small installs and adjustments into one efficient service day.

Homeowner checklist.

Use this before requesting an estimate. Better photos and better context make the first quote conversation much more useful.

Before you book
  • Walk the home in daylight and at night with lights on.
  • Open and close every door, drawer, window, and gate.
  • Run water at sinks, tubs, showers, and exterior hose bibs.
  • Look across walls from the side, not only straight on.
  • Save all builder communication and repair dates.
Estimate tip

Send context, not just close-ups.

Include one wide room or exterior photo, one close-up, measurements if possible, the city, when it started, and whether it changes with weather or use.

Decision rule

Do not pay twice for the same symptom.

If the first repair failed quickly, the second plan should explain why it failed and what will be different this time.

Frequently asked questions.

Short answers for homeowners deciding whether to clean, repair, rebuild, or call a specialist first.

What should be on a new construction punch list?

Visible defects, operation problems, missing parts, misalignment, damaged finishes, caulk gaps, paint misses, tile issues, and anything that does not function as expected.

Should I hire someone before the builder warranty expires?

It can help to have a second set of eyes, especially for finish details and maintenance items. Warranty decisions still depend on the builder agreement.

Are drywall cracks normal in a new DFW home?

Small settlement cracks can happen, but document them. Wider, growing, or offset cracks deserve more attention.

Can ELF handle items the builder will not fix?

Yes. After warranty items are sorted, ELF can help with owner upgrades, small repairs, installations, adjustments, and finish details.

Want a practical next step?

Send photos and your city. ELF will help identify whether this is maintenance, repair, or a larger scope before you spend money in the wrong place.
