Texas Problem Guide - Storm damage - DFW

North Texas storm repairs need triage before cosmetic work begins. Stop water first.

North Texas storms can leave a house with a confusing mix of problems: fence panels down, exterior trim damaged, water stains on ceilings, swollen baseboards, loose flashing, door leaks, and debris impact marks. The right response is not to patch everything immediately. It is to stop active water, document damage, sort urgent repairs from cosmetic repairs, and then rebuild cleanly.

Start with the real cause.

Storm damage is messy because several trades can be involved. A ceiling stain may come from roof, HVAC, window, siding, or plumbing triggered by wind-driven rain. A fallen fence may reveal rotten posts that were already weak. Exterior paint failure may be storm exposure plus old caulk. Good repair work begins by separating cause from symptom.

ELF handles practical repair and finish work after the immediate hazard is controlled: drywall patches, texture, paint, trim, doors, caulk, fence sections, and punch-list repairs. When roof, electrical, structural, or active water issues are present, those need the right specialist first.

Field note: Take photos before moving debris when it is safe. Documentation helps homeowners remember the sequence and can support conversations with insurance or other contractors.

Choose the right level of repair.

Most home problems have three possible paths: maintain, repair, or rebuild. Picking the wrong tier is how small projects get expensive.

Emergency stabilization

Tarp, secure, shut off, dry out, or block access before finish repair.

Damage contained

Multiple systems

DFW homes have their own repair patterns because of clay soil, heat, storms, hard water, fast construction, and HOA communities.

Local planning notes
  • Hail, wind, and sudden heavy rain are normal North Texas repair triggers.
  • Frisco, McKinney, Denton, Plano, and Allen homes often need fence and exterior trim work after wind events.
  • Interior water stains should be treated as symptoms until the entry point is confirmed.
When to slow down

If the problem involves active water, suspected hazardous material, structural movement, electrical risk, or a rule-controlled exterior change, pause the cosmetic work until the cause and approval path are clear.

How to handle it cleanly.

This is the field sequence that keeps a home repair from becoming a repeat repair.

Make safe

Stop active water, secure loose items, and avoid unsafe electrical or structural areas.

Document damage

Photograph wide views, close-ups, dates, and affected rooms before cleanup when safe.

Find the source

Confirm roof, window, exterior, plumbing, or HVAC source before cosmetic repairs.

Repair in layers

Exterior shell, drying, drywall, texture, paint, trim, and final cleanup.

Homeowner checklist.

Use this before requesting an estimate. Better photos and better context make the first quote conversation much more useful.

Before you book
  • Do not paint a ceiling stain until the source is fixed and the area is dry.
  • Photograph fence posts, not just fallen panels.
  • Check attic or ceiling access after wind-driven rain if safe.
  • Replace swollen MDF trim rather than caulking over it.
  • Keep receipts and repair notes together.
Estimate tip

Send context, not just close-ups.

Include one wide room or exterior photo, one close-up, measurements if possible, the city, when it started, and whether it changes with weather or use.

Decision rule

Do not pay twice for the same symptom.

If the first repair failed quickly, the second plan should explain why it failed and what will be different this time.

Frequently asked questions.

Short answers for homeowners deciding whether to clean, repair, rebuild, or call a specialist first.

What should I repair first after a storm?

Active water, safety hazards, and loose exterior items come first. Cosmetic drywall and paint should wait until the source is fixed and materials are dry.

Can a ceiling stain be painted over?

Only after the leak is fixed, the material is dry, and the stain is primed correctly. Soft or damaged drywall should be replaced.

Do fallen fence panels always require full fence replacement?

No. Sometimes a post reset or section repair is enough. If several posts are rotten or moving, replacement may be more practical.

Can ELF help with insurance claims?
