Start with the real decision.

Wood floors can look tired long before they need replacement. This guide explains which signs point to normal finish wear and which signs suggest moisture, movement, or damage under the surface.

Refinishing works when there is enough wood layer left and the problem is mostly in the finish. It does not solve active moisture, severe cupping, unstable boards, or thin veneer on many engineered products.

In DFW, slab moisture, humidity swings, pet damage, sunlight, and cleaning habits all affect floor life. The right plan starts by identifying the floor type and thickness.

Field note: If the same issue has returned more than once, solve the cause before spending money on the finish.

Homeowner checklist.

Use this before buying materials, starting DIY work, or asking for an estimate.

Checklist
  • Find a floor vent or threshold to inspect plank thickness.
  • Photograph the worst wear and the average wear separately.
  • Check for cupping with light across the boards.
  • Avoid steam mops on wood floors.
  • Use rugs and pads in high-traffic sunlit zones.
Estimate tip

Send context, not only close-ups.

Include one wide photo, one close-up, rough measurements, the city, when the issue started, and what has already been tried.

Decision rule

Protect the expensive layer.

When water, structure, substrate, code, or finish quality is involved, solve that layer before spending on visible finishes.

Frequently asked questions.

Short answers for deciding whether this is DIY, maintenance, repair, or a professional project.

Can engineered hardwood be refinished?

Sometimes. It depends on the wear layer thickness and product construction.

Does cupping go away after sanding?

Not safely if moisture is active. Moisture should be corrected before sanding.

Can pet stains be sanded out?

Some can, but deep black stains may require board replacement.

When is replacement better?

Replacement is better when the floor is too thin, unstable, heavily water damaged, or mismatched beyond repair.
